    About my renewed passion

    I used to enjoy biking a lot during my college days, especially until I got a motorcycle. Recently, I’ve rekindled my love for biking, and it feels so great to reconnect with it! First, let’s talk about my equipment. I ride an affordable bike, so I usually go to places nearby where I can bring it. It’s an e-bike, which really helps me keep going, even when I feel tired. It’s such a joyful way to stay active and enjoy the outdoors!

    Now that I’ve documented my bike, you will understand why I picked the following routes to ride. All of them are of easy to moderate difficulty. Not anything that is physically challenging.

    My first ride for the season was at Duke Farm. It offers over 18 miles of scenic trails through meadows, woodlands, lakes, etc. I should agree, I’ve never explored the entire 18 miles, as some of the roads aren’t accessible to the public most of the time.

    I will reference the D&R Canal Trail repeatedly in this document. This canal spans across New Jersey and extends approximately 70 miles. Cycling along the D&R Canal offers one of the top riding experiences in central Jersey. For those interested, it is possible to cycle the entire 70-mile length. However, there are multiple trails/loops along this canal, and my plan is to explore each and every one of them.

    My first ride was around the Six Mile Run Reservoir Site. If you would see the map, my ride was along the red trail (Creak Trail).

    We did a ride in the Loantaka Brook Reservation. The park offers a paved path that is around five miles long. The park spans over 850 acres and features almost 10 miles of trails. However, as mentioned earlier, I checked out only the paved path(s), which is around 5 miles. The trails are multifunctional, and you’ll see people cycling, jogging, hiking, horseback riding, and more.

    Next, I had the pleasure of exploring one of the beautiful Loop Trails. I enjoyed a lovely, shorter bike ride from Stockton to Lambertville. It was truly one of the most scenic routes, offering breathtaking views. Although it’s a brief journey, there’s a special section where you’ll ride right between the canal and the Raritan River, making it even more memorable. Both Stockton and Lambertville are charming, welcoming villages that visitors love to explore.

    I enjoy biking along the D&R Canal because the route follows an active canal, away from traffic, with shaded woods and a peaceful environment. That’s why I return to this canal for another ride, starting from the intersection of Manville Causeway and Weston Canal Rd.

    I mentioned the Loop Trails before. If you look at that map, my next adventure was from Frenchtown to Bulls Island, then to Lumberville, Uhlerstown, and back to Frenchtown. It was about 19 miles, and my bike wasn’t too happy about it. Since I was riding on a gravel road, I had to use the battery more often. My bike is rated to go up to 20 miles on a full charge, but it almost ran out of power halfway through, so I had to pedal the remaining 7 to 9 miles. The biking path in NJ was quite good, but in Pennsylvania, it wasn’t as pleasant. The bike path in PA was barely six inches wide, so I had to keep my focus on the road the whole time.

    In early May 2026, we had an unusually hot day in the mid-northeast USA and took the opportunity to explore the bike trail at Sandy Hook Beach. This multi-use trail spans about 8 to 9 miles along the scenic beaches. However, on our visit, we only rode roughly 5 miles due to construction in some parts of the island.

    Columbia Trail

    This time, I didn’t complete the entire trail. It’s a pretty manageable ride, about 15 miles each way. We chose to split it into two parts, starting with the northern section to make the adventure even more enjoyable. First, we visited a coffee shop that is across the trail. It’s a cozy coffee shop with a wonderful atmosphere. As shown in the picture, the trail passes through mature deciduous and evergreen forests that form a canopy blocking the sun. We biked south and stopped when we reached farm and pastureland. I’m told the southern part of the trail is more scenic. I need to explore it soon.

    🚴‍♂️ Riding the Henry Hudson Trail: Atlantic Section Review

    The Atlantic section of the Henry Hudson Trail offers a unique mix of scenic bay views and urban challenges. Starting your ride at 22 Gerard Avenue in Aberdeen Township, New Jersey, places you right at a key trailhead near Fireman’s Park (often associated with Oakshades Park), which provides convenient parking and access to the 9.7-mile northernmost segment that runs parallel to Route 36 toward Atlantic Highlands.

    The Ride Experience: Pavement and Roots

    For the majority of the journey, the trail is flat and paved, making it accessible for riders of various skill levels. The surface is generally well-maintained as it winds through residential backyards, small business areas, and wooded sections. However, consistent with your experience, many riders note that the final mile or so approaching the eastern end can be significantly rougher. In this section, tree roots often push up through the asphalt, creating bumps and “toe-clipper” hazards that disrupt an otherwise smooth ride. While the trail is officially asphalt throughout, age and root growth have left parts of the eastern end in need of resurfacing.

    Navigating Road Crossings

    One of the most frequent complaints about this specific section is the high number of road crossings. The trail frequently intersects with busy local streets, requiring riders to stop at traffic signals or crosswalks every few blocks. While these measures are designed for safety, they make it difficult to maintain a consistent cadence or speed. Riders often report that traffic does not always yield as expected, turning what should be a leisurely cruise into a stop-and-go workout. This fragmentation is particularly challenging for families with young children or those looking for a continuous aerobic ride.

    Weather Factors: The Sea Breeze

    Because the trail roughly parallels the shoreline and crosses various inlets and marshy areas, it is highly exposed to the elements. The sea breeze is a defining characteristic of the ride. On calm days, this provides a refreshing coolness, especially during the for those seeking summer months. However, when the wind is strong, riding against the breeze can be physically demanding, turning the flat 10-mile stretch into a grueling leg workout. Conversely, a tailwind can make the return trip exceptionally fast. Riders are advised to check wind conditions before heading out, as the lack of tree cover in certain sections leaves you fully exposed to the coastal winds.

    Scenic Highlights

    Despite the pavement issues and traffic interruptions, the trail offers rewarding scenery. As you pedal eastward, you will cross wooden bridges over tidal marshes and catch glimpses of Sandy Hook Bay. On clear days, the trail affords spectacular views of the New York City skyline in the distance, particularly around the 3.3-mile mark from the Matawan/Aberdeen end. The route transitions from industrial and residential zones to more open, scenic wetlands, providing a diverse backdrop that keeps the ride interesting even when the pavement quality dips.
